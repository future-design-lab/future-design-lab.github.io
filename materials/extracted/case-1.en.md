Case Library 1: Embodied Experience & Interaction Design

This case library focuses on the theme of "Embodied Experience and Interaction Design": when the body's familiarity breaks down, how can we re-establish the connection between people and the world through structured symbolic behaviors? It collects real-world projects and artistic practices concerned with "embodied experience," "sensory reconstruction," "AI interaction," and related directions, providing reference cases for designing body–environment–material relationships in Martian and outer-space contexts.

**EI-01｜Sound of Ikebana／Zero Gravity Art**

Description: This project was conducted jointly by Kyoto University, artist Naoko Tosa, and others, studying the behavior of "fluid art" in microgravity / zero-gravity environments. Under microgravity conditions simulated by free-fall apparatus or parabolic flight, sound vibrations (for example, a "newborn's cry") are used to excite pigments / fluids, and high-speed cameras record the new forms they take in zero-gravity or near-zero-gravity environments. In addition, "Zero Gravity Art" also built an immersive experience installation that simulates the floating sensation of weightlessness, allowing participants to experience the feeling of a "floating state" and "the loss of bodily support."

Insight: When the body is no longer governed by the conventional support logic of "ground → standing," the relationships among fluids, pigments, sound, and the body are rearranged: fluids splash, float, and diffuse due to weightlessness, and their generated forms detach from the traditional architecture of floor / wall / ceiling. The participant's body likewise shifts from "movement → positioning" to "floating → disorientation"—design/art is no longer based on "human support behavior under gravity," but instead turns toward perceptual construction in a "support-free / floating state."

Technology: Sensing Technology: monitoring sound vibrations and fluid motion.; Lighting Control: high-speed photography and lighting to capture fluid forms.; Audio System: using sound vibrations to excite fluid forms.; Immersive Technology: an experience installation simulating the floating state.

Design Object: Installation: an immersive zero-gravity floating experience installation.; Interactive System: a multisensory feedback system of sound–fluid–vision.; Soundscape: sound-centered triggering of visual generation.

Design Method: Embodiment: using the floating experience installation to let the audience feel the state of "loss of support" at the bodily level.; Simulation: using free-fall and parabolic flight on Earth to simulate zero gravity.; Synesthesia: sound vibrations trigger visual fluid changes, merging hearing with vision.; Generative: the forms that fluids take under sound-driven excitation are jointly generated by algorithm and natural forces.; Ritualization: using sounds symbolizing the origin of life, such as a "newborn's cry," to trigger artistic generation, carrying a ritualistic metaphor.

Future Impact: Provides a paradigm for art and experience design in future space and outer-space environments: when people are in zero-gravity or extremely low-gravity environments, how should design construct the relationship among body, environment, and material.

Principle: Changes in fluid behavior under the absence of gravity; perceptual/positional disorientation after the loss of bodily support.

Source: KYOTO University / Naoko Tosa team papers and project reports. Specifically, the paper "Creation of Fluid Art under Microgravity Using Free-fall."; https://repository.kulib.kyoto-u.ac.jp/dspace/bitstream/2433/277775/1/978-3-031-05434-1_23.pdf

**EI-02｜Space/Craft**

Description: This project was led by the Space Exploration Initiative at MIT Media Lab, with artist Alexis Hope in charge. The experiment involved using a hot glue gun to "draw in the air" and create "3D drawings" inside a container during parabolic flight simulating zero-gravity / low-gravity conditions—lines that could not freely float into form under normal gravity float and take shape into a new sculptural language under weightlessness. https://www.explore-space.mit.edu/catalog/space-craft?utm_source=chatgpt.com In an environment with "no ground" and "no up / down," all the relationships among bodily movement, tool movement, and material generation are reconstructed.

Insight: After the body loses its "gravitational anchoring," its movements, material interactions, and structural generation all detach from the traditional "ground-volume" structure; the design language must free itself from "gravitational reference." This provides ideas for "handcraft / craft / sculpture" in future space missions: when the body cannot stand firmly and tools cannot be stably positioned, design must support floating, disorientation, and three-dimensional fluid / linear extension.

Technology: Immersive Technology: simulating a zero-gravity environment.; IoT / Smart Environment: used to monitor and control the floating creation environment.; Sensing Technology: tracking material and hand movements.; Data Interface: recording creation data and the evolution of forms in real time.

Design Object: Installation: a floating sculpture and creation experiment system.; Architecture: a floating space concept for space-based art creation.; Interactive System: a real-time interaction system among tool–body–material.

Design Method: Embodiment: using the body's movement in weightlessness as the core generative method.; Transference: transferring Earth's "craft / handwork" into a zero-gravity context.; Generative: materials naturally form new three-dimensional structures under weightlessness.; Simulation: recreating the space environment through parabolic flight.; Contrast: creating new forms through the contrast between "Earth-gravity handwork" and "space-weightless handwork."

Future Impact: Could be extended into art spaces or interactive experiences in future space bases and orbital living modules. It provides a paradigm for design systems in which "gravity is no longer the default condition": furniture, craft, interaction, and bodily movement all need to be rebuilt.

Principle: A creative process freed from gravitational reference; the relationship among tool-body-material in a floating state.

Source: MIT Media Lab official project page; https://www.explore-space.mit.edu/catalog/space-craft

**EI-03｜Phalanstery Module**

Description: This is an architecture / installation design: a vertically rotating living space. The designer posits that "if in a zero-gravity / weightless state, people could use the walls, ceiling, and floor all as 'ground,'" that is, the relationship between planes and support is broken. https://bureau-spectacular.net/project/phalanstery-module/?utm_source=chatgpt.com The structure rotates slowly (once per hour), so that the wall, ceiling, and floor alternately become walkable surfaces, allowing one to experience a construction with "no fixed gravitational direction."

Insight: The body loses its gravitational anchoring: we are accustomed to "feet on the ground" and "sky overhead," but in weightlessness or free floating, this directional reference disappears. How design reconstructs "support / walking / positioning" thus becomes key. Architectural grammar must be rewritten: wall, floor, and ceiling are no longer constant roles; the "ground" may be on a wall or on the ceiling. Designers must consider the situation of weakened bodily support.

Technology: Robotic Interaction: controlling rotation and dynamic structural transformation.; Sensing Technology: monitoring spatial motion and body position.; IoT / Smart Environment: dynamic control of environment and structure.

Design Object: Architecture: a rotating residential structure.; Installation: as an experimental spatial installation.; Interactive System: dynamic rotation and bodily responses form interaction.

Design Method: Simulation: using the rotating structure to simulate changes in gravitational direction.; Embodiment: users experience the disorder of gravitational direction through bodily movement.; Mapping: the perceptual mapping between the building structure's rotation and gravitational direction.; Contrast: breaking the fixed logic of "floor/ceiling."; Narrativization: constructing a temporal narrative of "life rhythm" through the rhythm of rotation.

Future Impact: Provides conceptual ideas for the "groundless-reference" living environments required by space living modules, orbital stations, and lunar / Martian bases. Inspires designers to consider: in an environment where the direction toward the planet's center disappears, how are "furniture," "walking," and "support" to be redefined.

Principle: Support structures in zero gravity; the absence of bodily positional reference; reference confusion caused by rotation.

Source: Bureau Spectacular official project page & design commentary; https://bureau-spectacular.net/project/phalanstery-module/

**EI-04｜Kyoto University & Kajima Construction Co., Ltd. "Artificial Gravity Living Structure Research"**

Description: Japan's Kyoto University and Kajima Construction Co., Ltd. are collaborating to develop research on an "artificial gravity living facility": planning to build a rotating displacement structure that achieves conditions similar to Earth's 1G or higher gravity on the Moon / Mars or in orbit, in order to solve the problems of the human body's / growth's / support in low-gravity, weightless states. https://www.designboom.com/technology/the-glass-artificial-gravity-facility-kyoto-university-07-11-2022/?utm_source=chatgpt.com The research notes that in low-gravity environments, mammals may be unable to be born or grow normally, and humans living long-term in low gravity may be unable to stand normally after returning to Earth. Therefore, "it is essential to establish an artificial gravity living facility" as a core technology. https://www.designboom.com/technology/the-glass-artificial-gravity-facility-kyoto-university-07-11-2022/?utm_source=chatgpt.com

Insight: A direct shift from a "weightlessness / absence of gravity" scenario to a "restoration / substitution of support" scenario: after the body loses its familiar support mechanism (gravity), it is not only the experience that changes—physiological structural changes may also result. Design is therefore not only about "how to float," but about "how to establish new support."

Technology: Robotic Interaction: used for rotation and dynamic control.; IoT / Smart Environment: an environmental monitoring and gravity-simulation control system.; Sensing Technology: monitoring the human body's state and gravity parameters.; Data Interface: a real-time connection between the building's physical parameters and physiological data.

Design Object: Architecture: an artificial gravity living facility.; Interactive System: the interactive relationship of the human-space-gravity system.; Bio-design: environmental design related to the human body's physiological adaptation.

Design Method: Simulation: simulating artificial gravity through a centrifugal structure.; Embodiment: spatial design that takes the body's gravitational adaptation as the core consideration.; Generative: the system dynamically maintains the artificial gravity environment through rotation.; Intervention: directly intervening in human physiology and living conditions through architectural and technical means.; Mapping: the mathematical mapping among gravity intensity, spatial geometry, and rotation speed.

Future Impact: Provides a "gravity substitution theory + design" pathway for future long-term living on the Moon, Mars, and in orbital environments. It inspires designers and engineers: when the body's support system (i.e., gravity) is weakened, design must compensate for or transform the logic of support.

Principle: Centrifugal artificial gravity; the human body's gravitational adaptation; maintenance of bodily structure.

Source: Designboom report; https://www.designboom.com/technology/the-glass-artificial-gravity-facility-kyoto-university-07-11-2022/

**EI-07｜Seiko Mikami's Gravicells**

Description: Gravicells is an interactive installation completed by Japanese artist Seiko Mikami in 2004 in collaboration with architects, installed at YCAM (Yamaguchi Center for Arts and Media). In the installation, a 6×6-meter floor panel is covered with sensors that can sense the participant's weight, tilt, and speed, then generate projection distortions in real time. By exploring the role of "gravity" in the body, perception, and space, the work prompts participants to reflect on the relationship among bodily support, center of gravity, and motion and stillness.

Insight: Although not directly in a space environment, it is precisely "gravitational reference" that is taken as the object of design intervention: the body's familiar "standing," "balance," and "center of gravity" are scattered and reconstructed by sensors / projection. It embodies the direction of "sensory reconstruction": sensing the body's weight / tilt / motion-stillness through system feedback, allowing participants to re-recognize the disrupted familiar state of "my position in space."

Technology: Sensing Technology: weight and pressure sensors built into the floor panel.; Lighting Control: real-time projection distortion and light feedback.; Audio System: converting gravity data into sound elements.; Data Interface: a computing system that processes and transmits sensing signals.

Design Object: Installation: a floor-sensing and projection interactive installation.; Interactive System: a real-time-feedback body–space–image system.; Architecture: changing the body's perceived "ground" through spatial feedback.; Soundscape: the part where the sense of gravity is converted into audio signals.

Design Method: Embodiment: taking changes in body weight, tilt, and center of gravity as the core of interaction.; Mapping: mapping body motion data into projected images and sound changes.; Simulation: simulating the experience of "gravity anomaly" through visual/perceptual feedback.; Synesthesia: converting the body's mechanical signals into visual and sound feedback.; Intervention: actively disturbing the body's trust in gravity and balance, prompting perceptual reconstruction.

Future Impact: Although not directly a space-environment case, it provides a precursor for understanding the relationship between "the body's weightless reference" and "design participation." In future low- / zero-gravity environments, similar structures (sensing + feedback + bodily interaction) can be used to rebuild the connection among body, space, and material.

Principle: The body's balance / center-of-gravity reference is detected and fed back by a design system; the interactive installation as a medium connecting body and environment; the support mechanism is converted into a perceptual mechanism.

Source: Artist materials and installation introduction; https://www.ntticc.or.jp/en/feature/2008/Openspace2008/Works/gravicells.html

Technology: Sensing Technology: collecting physiological data such as movement, breathing, and electromyography.; Biofeedback: analyzing body signals in real time and generating feedback.; Lighting Control: projection and ambient light changes synchronized with the body's rhythm.; Audio System: sound feedback corresponding to the body's movement or breathing intensity.; Data Interface: integrating sensing inputs with multimodal outputs.

Design Method: Synesthesia: translating body signals (breathing, electromyography, movement) into visual and sound feedback.; Embodiment: using body data as the primary input, making the body a participant in environmental generation.; Mapping: establishing a real-time mapping among the body's physiological data, spatial projection, and soundscape.; Generative: the system generates new audiovisual symbols in real time according to bodily changes.; Intervention: blurring the boundary between body and interface, redefining the relationship between "bodily support" and "environmental feedback."

Future Impact: Can be transferred to space residence / orbital living scenarios: that is, the value of bodily movement in a floating environment does not disappear, but is extended through the interactive interface.
