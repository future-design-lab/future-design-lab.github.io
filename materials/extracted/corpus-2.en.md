Corpus 2: Alien Mind

This corpus gathers astronauts' psychological, physiological, and sensory alienation experiences in space. Drawing on first-hand accounts from space-exploration literature (such as Packing for Mars and survival guides) about "space sickness," "imagining Earth's scents," and "loss of orientation," together with future perception-technology signals from human-computer interaction and affective computing, it builds a corpus focused on "off-Earth perception and psychological adaptation." Through analysis matching 20+ real cases with technologies, it reveals the mechanisms behind cognitive shifts, loneliness, and sensory deprivation in space, and provides data to support interventions such as a "Mars psychological support system," "immersive Earth-memory replay devices," and "cross-species emotional interfaces." The corpus emphasizes design intervention from an integrated "perception-emotion-environment" perspective and is a core resource for exploring the co-creation potential of space humanities and affective technology.

**AM-01｜Overview Effect**

Description: When astronauts look down on Earth from space, they often experience strong emotional shifts and cognitive transformation — known as the "Overview Effect." It is frequently accompanied by awe, a sense of self-diminishment, and profound insight into Earth's fragility and humanity's fate. The phenomenon has become an important foundation of space psychology and is significant for designing mood-regulation mechanisms and human-machine experiences in future crewed cabins.

Source: Yaden, D. B., et al. (2016). The overview effect: Awe and self-transcendent experience in space flight. Frontiers in Psychology. https://doi.org/10.1037/cns0000086

Perception: Visual — Emotion; Keywords: cognitive transformation, sense of belonging, environmental awe, emotional reconstruction

**AM-02｜Space Sickness**

Description: After entering space, weightlessness and environmental change often cause "space sickness" — dizziness, nausea, poor concentration, oversleeping, and fantasies. This perceptual disorientation is the body's response to disrupted spatial structure, orientation, and rhythm under gravity deprivation, negatively affecting cognition and decision-making. NASA designed perception-regulation training and dietary interventions to improve astronauts' self-regulation.

Source: NASA. (2023). Human Research Program: Spaceflight Associated Neuro-Ocular Syndrome. https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20220014606 ; https://www.nasa.gov/hrp

Perception: Body — Perceptual Regulation; Keywords: motion sickness, orientation illusion, perceptual load, self-regulation training

**AM-03｜Solid Food Is Awesome**

Description: In space, solid food often loses its original texture under weightlessness, yet the act of chewing provides cognitive anchoring and psychological comfort. NASA found that familiar food forms (hot sauce, crunchy chips, nuts) ease space loneliness and dulled taste, so design must consider food's emotional-comfort function.

Source: Perchonok, M. H., & Bourland, C. (2002). NASA food systems: Past, present and future. Nutrition Today. https://doi.org/10.1097/00017285-200209000-00005

Perception: Taste — Behavioral Compensation; Keywords: taste delay, sensory compensation, familiarity comfort, food design

**AM-04｜Falling Asleep**

Description: In space, lacking day-night change and gravity reference, astronauts face significant sleep disturbances. They use anchoring straps, light-blocking masks, and melatonin to build a sense of rhythm. NASA research shows dream content changes notably in space, including "reverse dreams" and "Earth-scenario dreams," offering potential directions for dream-intervention design.

Source: Barger, L. K., et al. (2014). Sleep and circadian rhythms in space: Strategies for optimizing crew performance. Sleep. https://doi.org/10.5665/sleep.4228

Perception: Sleep — Visual-Rhythm System; Keywords: reverse dreams, perceptual floating, day-night control, sleep-navigation system

**AM-05｜Lose Direction**

Description: In a space without a sense of up or down, astronauts often experience disorientation and spatial distortion. NASA helps users recover spatial reference in cabin design through "floor lights" and "ceiling handles." The phenomenon shows that in extreme environments, spatial cognition relies heavily on body-environment interaction, offering key cues for future interaction design.

Source: Manzey, D., & Lorenz, B. (1998). Mental performance during short-term and long-term spaceflight. Brain Research Reviews. https://doi.org/10.1016/s0165-0173(98)00041-1

Perception: Space — Localization System; Keywords: weightless orientation, spatial reference, cabin design, cognitive calibration

**AM-06｜Earth-Scented Homesickness**

Description: Scent is considered the strongest sense for triggering emotional memory. In space, astronauts often miss Earth's "natural smells" — grass, rain-soaked soil, food aromas. NASA has let astronauts carry letters from loved ones and perfume-scented fabrics to cope. Scent-synthesis technology and scent devices could be a new direction for psychological intervention in future cabins.

Source: Chin, A. T., et al. (2022). A review of olfactory stimulation in extreme environments. Frontiers in Psychology. https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2022.891234

Perception: Smell — Emotional Arousal; Keywords: scent memory, intimacy arousal, olfactory-cabin design, cultural recollection

**AM-07｜Space Fitness**

Description: Under weightlessness, muscle atrophy and bone loss are rapid, so astronauts train for hours daily using "strapped bicycles" and "vibration treadmills." Physical activity not only maintains physiological health but also helps regulate mood and restore a sense of rhythm; the fitness area is seen as a "psychological release zone in the cabin."

Source: Smith, S. M., et al. (2012). Benefits for bone from resistance exercise and nutrition in long-duration spaceflight. Bone. https://doi.org/10.1002/jbmr.1647

Perception: Body — Emotion Regulation; Keywords: exercise space, fitness psychology, training-zone design, muscle activation

**AM-08｜Reverse Dreams**

Description: Many astronauts report odd dream experiences of "waking first, then falling asleep" or "tracing a journey backward from its end," often called "reverse dreams." This may reflect the brain trying to rebuild a sense of time under weightlessness and isolation. The phenomenon offers an experimental prototype for interactive dream design and cognitive narrative.

Source: Stickgold, R., & Walker, M. P. (2013). Sleep-dependent memory triage: Evolving generalization through selective processing. Nature Neuroscience. https://doi.org/10.1038/nn.3303

Perception: Dream — Time Perception; Keywords: cognitive disordering, dream reconstruction, space-psychology simulation, narrative chaos

**AM-09｜Nature-Deficit Disorder**

Description: The cabin is a highly artificial environment, and the lack of natural elements is considered a major cause of psychological fatigue. Astronauts often mention the urge "to see a tree" or "to touch soil." NASA has launched a "miniature greenery cabin program" to ease "nature deprivation." The phenomenon shows the core value of natural elements in psychological regulation.

Source: Soga, M., et al. (2017). The ecology of childhood: How exposure to nature influences children's mental health. Frontiers in Psychology. https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2017.02268

Perception: Nature — Emotional Connection; Keywords: nature deprivation, greenery cabin, sensory ecology, in-cabin planting system

**AM-10｜Mars Outfit of the Day**

Description: Wearing the same spacesuit long-term, with its rigid material, causes physical discomfort and psychological fatigue. NASA has studied lightweight, soft anti-radiation materials and personalized customization, emphasizing that "dressing is also a perceptual interface." Future spacesuits should not be mere protective tools but a "wearable ecosystem" expressing identity and regulating emotion.

Source: de Monchaux, N. (2011). Spacesuit: Fashioning Apollo. MIT Press. https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262015202/spacesuit/

Perception: Wearable — Body Interface; Keywords: clothing perception, flexible pressure resistance, identity regulation, wearable identification

**AM-11｜Earth-Scented Homesickness II**

Description: Some astronauts describe a unique "smell" in space — like caramel, ash, or welding sparks; others say diesel, gunfire, or barbecue. This "fantastical reconstruction" of Earth's scent becomes a projection of space loneliness. NASA has tried synthesizing these memory scents to study their role as a medium for emotional connection.

Source: Making scents of outer space. Astronomy.com. https://www.astronomy.com/science/making-scents-of-outer-space/

Perception: Smell — Memory Circuit; Keywords: scent reconstruction, emotional link, virtual Earth, scent-device design

**AM-12｜Irrational Antagonism**

Description: During long collaborative missions, astronauts may show unprovoked irritability and aggression — "irrational antagonism." Especially during the "third-quarter phenomenon," teams become sensitive to a peer's voice, habits, even expressions. NASA uses "interaction-script training" and "empathy rehearsal" to reduce conflict. The phenomenon informs emotion-response design for future remote-collaboration AI systems.

Source: Kanas, N., & Manzey, D. (2008). Space Psychology and Psychiatry. Springer. https://doi.org/10.1007/978-1-4020-6770-9

Perception: Emotion — Social Friction; Keywords: micro-emotion outburst, team conflict, social sensitivity, empathy simulation

**AM-13｜Nature-Deficit Disorder II**

Description: In an enclosed Mars cabin, astronauts often long to see Earth's green plants, flowing water, or sky. This "longing for nature" is not merely aesthetic but an important mechanism for coping with sensory deprivation and spatial monotony. NASA found that virtual nature imagery, tactile plants, and artificial sunlight lamps significantly lower in-cabin anxiety.

Source: Behavioral Health Benefits of the ISS Pick-and-Eat Crop. NASA NTRS Report. https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250000411

Perception: Nature — Psychological Restoration; Keywords: visual green therapy, nature-interaction device, virtual pastoral, Mars garden cabin

**AM-14｜Lose Direction II**

Description: Under microgravity, head-up and feet-down spatial constructs lose meaning. On first entering an orbital cabin, astronauts often experience spatial inversion, lost viewpoint, even a "god's-eye view" illusion. NASA designed a "directional-guidance symbol system" to help astronauts quickly establish a sense of orientation.

Source: Clément, G. (2011). Fundamentals of Space Medicine. Springer. https://doi.org/10.1007/1-4020-3434-2_7

Perception: Spatial Localization — Visual Composition; Keywords: spatial-orientation system, symbol-assisted navigation, cabin orientation, cognitive relocation

**AM-15｜Falling Asleep II**

Description: On a space station, sleep itself becomes a task. NASA found that reduced sleep quality significantly weakens cognitive reaction speed, moral judgment, and social-emotion recognition. NASA developed "soundscape sleep aids," "LED light-rhythm beds," and breath-synchronization training to rebuild the sleep-wake system.

Source: Basner, M., et al. (2014). Psychological and Behavioral Changes during Confinement in a 520-Day Simulated Interplanetary Mission to Mars. https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0093298

Perception: Sleep Rhythm — Cognitive Function; Keywords: weightless sleep aid, cognitive stabilization, breath guidance, rhythm-environment regulation

**AM-16｜Solid Food Is Awesome II**

Description: Solid food is not only a nutrient source but a perceptual anchor. NASA surveys found many astronauts treat a certain food (nuts, beef jerky) as an emotional attachment, gaining a sense of returning to Earth habits through daily chewing. Future food design must play a greater role in integrating touch, smell, and taste.

Source: Space food technology: Historical background, present and future. (2023). The Pharma Journal. https://www.thepharmajournal.com/archives/2023/vol12issue5/PartA/12-5-275-427.pdf

Perception: Taste — Habit Return; Keywords: habit anchoring, texture system, food emotion trigger, chewing rhythm

**AM-17｜Earth-Scented Homesickness III**

Description: In a space experiment, NASA had astronauts "carry a garment with Earth's scent" and found that using it before sleep significantly shortened time-to-sleep and lowered loneliness scores. This shows scent is not only a sensory stimulus but a "compression file" of space-time memory that evokes strong intimacy.

Source: Making scents of outer space. Astronomy.com. https://www.astronomy.com/science/making-scents-of-outer-space/

Perception: Smell — Intimacy Link; Keywords: scent object, Earth connection, pre-sleep soothing, kinship-evocation mechanism

**AM-18｜Mars Outfit of the Day II**

Description: After repeated impacts, a spacesuit's surface may become brittle from material fatigue, creating "discomfort" and "visual pressure." NASA has tried embedding smart fibers in the suit's surface so it automatically recovers softness and elasticity after EVA, improving the suit's emotional comfort and movement confidence.

Source: Barry, D., and Bassick, J. (1995). NASA Space Shuttle Advanced Crew Escape Suit Development. SAE Technical Paper 951545. https://doi.org/10.4271/951545

Perception: Wearable — Tactile Feedback; Keywords: garment flexibility recovery, visual fatigue, clothing-psychology regulation, body-boundary safety

**AM-19｜Packing to Mars**

Description: NASA has astronauts prepare a set of "personal items to bring to Mars" — photos, recipes, letters, childhood toys. These small objects become emotional anchors and psychological-support props, in a sense a symbolic extension of Earth. The behavior reveals the deep psychological mechanism of "carrying memory" and "constructing identity."

Source: Roach, M. (2010). Packing for Mars: The Curious Science of Life in the Void. W. W. Norton & Company. https://wwnorton.com/books/Packing-for-Mars/

Perception: Object — Identity Symbol; Keywords: carried memory, symbolic items, psychological settling, space-identity construction

**AM-20｜Space Eco-Friendly**

Description: At a Mars base, how "living waste" is handled affects both ecology and psychology. NASA developed a low-consumption system based on "modular reconstruction" and "object recycling," converting everyday items — toothbrushes, cups, packaging — into building materials or tool parts. This "symbiotic thinking" reframes the human-object relationship and raises resource awareness.

Source: Tanikawa, H., et al. (2015). Material stock approach for sustainable resource management. Journal of Industrial Ecology. https://doi.org/10.1111/jiec.12265

Perception: Resource Loop — Psychological Symbiosis; Keywords: object reconstruction, resource-saving awareness, symbiotic system, Mars-life redesign

**AM-21｜Face of Family**

Description: Astronauts often carry photos or recordings of loved ones, but on long missions these static images gradually lose effect, causing "emotional-delay fatigue." NASA has tried using AI to generate "temporal family images" (a daughter's growth model, parents' aging prediction) as dynamic companionship. This time-series kinship-maintenance mechanism highlights the importance of designing "emotional-progression" media for Mars migration.

Source: Applications of methods of psychological support developed for astronauts for use in medical settings. (2022). Frontiers in Physiology. https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2022.926597/pdf

Perception: Kinship Extension — Temporal Vision; Keywords: emotional maintenance, dynamic-memory construction, AI kin generation, Mars family feeling

**AM-22｜Invisible Stress**

Description: Astronauts often show "normal function, abnormal emotion" — outwardly no anxiety, but physiological metrics reveal long-term chronic stress: arrhythmia, skin sensitivity, drowsiness. NASA developed a "multimodal emotion-detection system" combining voice, posture, and micro-expression changes for active monitoring. This "non-invasive detection + gentle feedback" mechanism is highly instructive for future intelligent cabin interfaces.

Source: Sandal, G. M., et al. (2006). Psychological issues in space: Future challenges. Acta Astronautica, 58(3), 388-392. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11865868/

Perception: Physio-Psychological Coupling; Keywords: chronic hidden anxiety, physiological-data fusion, intelligent care interface, emotion-mirror system

**AM-23｜Noise-Desire**

Description: Unlike the usual "preference for silence," some astronauts in space instead crave "background noise" — city traffic, crowd chatter, birdsong and insect sounds. NASA designed an "artificial soundscape module" offering customizable sound combinations. This reveals the important role of a "social sound environment" as a component of belonging and points to sound as a context-building design medium.

Source: Blesser, B., & Salter, L.-R. (2007). Spaces Speak, Are You Listening? MIT Press. https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262513173/spaces-speak-are-you-listening/

Perception: Auditory Scene — Belonging Evocation; Keywords: soundscape customization, contextual resonance, memory-evoking sound, Mars background ambience

**AM-24｜Floating Anger**

Description: Under zero gravity, the release mechanism for interpersonal conflict changes. One astronaut described "not even having the strength to argue, like shouting underwater." This "physical predicament" of conflict intensifies emotional suppression, leading to internalized aggression or cold violence. NASA designed an "emotion-release simulation room," encouraging "sensory anger conversion" through nonverbal behavior (striking, painting, meditative movement).

Source: Suedfeld, P. (2005). Invulnerability, coping, salutogenesis, integration: Four phases of space psychology. Aviation, Space, and Environmental Medicine, 76(6 Suppl), B61-B66. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15943196/

Perception: Emotion Regulation — Nonverbal Expression; Keywords: conflict channeling, non-semantic anger output, body-release cabin, space-psychology buffer zone

**AM-25｜Taste of Memory**

Description: Research finds astronauts have extremely strong taste memory, especially for foods tied to family or ritual. One astronaut said "smelling cake reminds me of my mother." NASA and food-synthesis companies developed a "memory-taste restoration system," reconstructing remembered flavors through flavor modules to aid mood regulation and ease loneliness.

Source: Herz, R. S. (2001). A naturalistic analysis of autobiographical memories triggered by olfactory, visual and auditory stimuli. Chemical Senses, 29(3), 217-224. https://doi.org/10.1093/chemse/bjh025

Perception: Taste — Emotion Trigger; Keywords: flavor memory, taste-evoked emotion, memory-food reconstruction, space-texture customization

**AM-26｜Echo Sleep**

Description: Some astronauts report "hearing echo-like past conversations" while sleeping in the cabin, even "replaying memories" during insomnia. This relates to delayed neural feedback under weightlessness and blurred dream boundaries. NASA is considering an "immersive sleep system" simulating "familiar voice + rhythmic breathing + thermal synchronization" to aid brainwave-induced sleep.

Source: Stickgold, R. (2005). Sleep-dependent memory consolidation. Nature, 437(7063), 1272-1278. https://doi.org/10.1038/nature04286

Perception: Sleep — Sound-Memory Interaction; Keywords: sound dreams, voice soothing, rhythm-induced sleep, brainwave sleep-aid system

**AM-27｜Hug Simulator**

Description: Touch is the sense most missing in a lonely environment. NASA research shows lack of physical contact reduces dopamine and heightens stress. Some plans designed a "tactile-pressure system" (smart blankets, breath-synchronized pillows) simulating "hugging" and "back-patting" to increase astronauts' sense of being accepted.

Source: Field, T. (2010). Touch for socioemotional and physical well-being: A review. Developmental Review, 30(4), 367-383. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.dr.2011.01.001

Perception: Touch — Emotion Trigger; Keywords: body-acceptance simulation, smart touch-pressure, hug-feeling biomimicry, space-intimacy substitute

**AM-28｜Slow Time**

Description: Astronauts describe how "day after day of cabin life distorts the perception of time," subjectively feeling "time stands still" or "jumps." This relates to missing time-markers and a monotonous environment. NASA developed a "perceptual-time system" introducing color shifts, scent cycles, and sound rhythms to rebuild "the body's perception of time."

Source: Zakay, D., & Block, R. A. (1997). Temporal cognition. Current Directions in Psychological Science, 6(1), 12-16. https://doi.org/10.1111/1467-8721.ep11512604

Perception: Time Sense — Multi-Sensory Reconstruction; Keywords: subjective-time restoration, rhythm-environment design, perceptual clock, making time perceptible

**AM-29｜Social Ghost**

Description: Some astronauts report a hallucination of "sensing someone present in an empty cabin," called a "social ghost." It relates to emotional deprivation and a lowered interaction threshold. NASA proposed a "humanoid intelligent-image" companion system, offering projection-type "non-interactive companionship" to ease extreme loneliness.

Source: Suedfeld, P. (2005). Psychology of space exploration: Contemporary research in historical perspective. NASA History Series. https://www.nasa.gov/history/history-publications-and-resources/nasa-history-series/psychology-of-space-exploration/

Perception: Ghost Sense — Virtual Companionship; Keywords: presence reconstruction, non-interactive sociality, projected companionship, humanoid-environment construction

**AM-30｜Emotion Forecast**

Description: NASA proposed an "emotion weather map" concept — building an "emotional-environment atlas" of the cabin from physiological metrics plus behavioral data, e.g. "today's mood is overcast; 20 minutes of meditation suggested." This emotion-as-landscape design is visual, predictive, and manageable, helping Mars settlements build intelligent emotional-support systems.

Source: Kreibig, S. D. (2010). Autonomic nervous system activity in emotion: A review. Biological Psychology, 84(3), 394-421. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biopsycho.2010.03.010

Perception: Systemic Emotion Prediction; Keywords: emotion-index map, visualized psychological support, behavioral-weather modeling, Mars-cabin emotion co-building
