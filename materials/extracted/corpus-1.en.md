Corpus 1: Independent from Earth

This corpus focuses on the key technology signals needed to support long-term settlement on Mars. It spans three stages — short-term survival (air, water, power), mid-term sustainment (food, radiation protection, medicine), and long-term self-sufficiency (energy loops, ecological reconstruction, reproduction) — corresponding to humanity's progression on an alien world from "landing and surviving" to "stable living" and on to "ecological autonomy." By selecting future-signal entries from frontier fields such as biotechnology, energy engineering, and nanosystems, the corpus provides theoretical grounding and design inspiration for building Martian habitation systems. It is especially suited to technology-scenario construction, prototype development, and situational analysis in design futures. The corpus emphasizes technological adaptability and systems integration under space conditions, making it an important resource for prototyping future Martian habitats.

**Corpus 1: "Independent from Earth" — Technology Signals for Mars Settlement (First 10)**

**IE-01｜Mars Oxygen ISRU Experiment (MOXIE)**

Description: In Mars's thin carbon-dioxide atmosphere, MOXIE converts CO₂ into oxygen via solid-oxide electrolysis — the first time humans have generated breathable air and propellant oxidizer on another planet through In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU). The technology directly supports the most critical air-generation need in the "short-term / survival essentials" tier, providing key assurance for the early survival of a Mars base.

Source: Hecht, M., et al. (2021). Mars Oxygen ISRU Experiment (MOXIE): Preparing for human Mars exploration. Space Science Reviews, 217(1), Article 9. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11214-020-00782-8

Category: Life Support Systems; Keywords: oxygen generation, Martian breathing, survival assurance

**IE-02｜Solar + Fuel-Cell Hybrid Power System**

Description: Mars has weak solar radiation, a thin atmosphere, and a power system challenged by day-night temperature swings and dust storms. A hybrid system combining photovoltaics (PV) and airborne wind energy (AWE), with lithium-sulfur batteries and CO₂-compression storage, balances energy across day-night and seasonal cycles, providing stable power in the short-term stage. This hybrid is especially suited to the "short-term / survival essentials" tier, keeping habitat modules, communications, and air-cycling systems running continuously.

Source: Ouroumova, L., Witte, D., et al. (2021). Combined airborne wind and photovoltaic energy system for Martian habitats. arXiv. https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.09506

Category: Energy Systems; Keywords: energy hybridization, solar + fuel cell, base power supply

**IE-03｜Thermal-Control Insulation Materials & Passive Thermal Management**

Description: Mars has extreme day-night temperature swings and no Earth-like atmospheric buffer, so habitat modules and clothing must integrate advanced insulation materials and thermal-control systems. High-conductivity coatings, phase-change materials, or radiation-reflective coatings can maintain suitable temperatures in the "short-term / survival essentials" stage. Such systems are basic infrastructure for life-support equipment and human thermal balance.

Source: Zhong, Y., Wu, T., Han, Y., et al. (2025). Advancements in Mars habitation technologies and terrestrial simulation projects: A comprehensive review. Aerospace, 12(6), 510. https://doi.org/10.3390/aerospace12060510

Category: Thermal-Control Materials; Keywords: temperature regulation, cabin insulation, heat protection & dissipation

**IE-04｜Atmospheric Water Harvesting Membranes / MOF Water Capture**

Description: On Mars, water is scarce and precious. Porous metal-organic-framework (MOF) membranes can capture moisture from the thin atmosphere or polar ice for drinking water and recycling. This maps to the drinking-water need of the "mid-term / settlement sustainment" tier — a key step for a settlement to move from dependence on Earth resupply toward partial self-sufficiency.

Source: Maity, T., Saxena, A. (2024). Challenges and innovations in food and water availability for a sustainable Mars colonization. Life Sciences in Space Research, 42, 27-36. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.lssr.2024.03.008

Category: Water Systems; Keywords: atmospheric water harvesting, drinking-water acquisition, recycling

**IE-05｜Radiation-Protection Fiber-Composite System**

Description: Mars's surface receives far more cosmic and solar radiation than Earth, so long-term habitation must protect the body from radiation damage. Building habitat modules and spacesuits from high-hydrogen polymers, nanofiber-reinforced layers, and regolith-polymer composite shielding is a key technology in the "mid-term / settlement sustainment" tier for ensuring human health and long-duration residence.

Source: Zhong, Y., et al. (2025). Advancements in Mars habitation technologies and terrestrial simulation projects. Aerospace, 12(6), 510. https://doi.org/10.3390/aerospace12060510

**IE-11｜Low-Temperature CO₂-H₂O Electrolysis (Methane & Oxygen ISRU)**

Description: To meet the challenge of further boosting oxygen and methane output beyond the MOXIE payload on Perseverance, researchers proposed CO₂-H₂O electrolysis under Martian low-temperature conditions (around 255 K) to produce oxygen and methane simultaneously. The system supplies both breathing oxygen and rocket propellant — a key energy-resource closed-loop step in the "long-term / self-sufficiency" tier.

Source: Shahid, M., Chambers, B., & Sankarasubramanian, S. (2024). Methane and oxygen from energy-efficient, low-temperature in-situ resource utilization enables missions to Mars. arXiv. https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2404.00800
