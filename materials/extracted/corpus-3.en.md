Corpus 3: Hidden Twins

The "Hidden Twins" corpus systematically collects representative cases in which space technology has been translated into everyday terrestrial products, spanning healthcare, environmental protection, food, apparel, safety, architecture, and education. Each case not only traces the origin of the technology (such as spacecraft thermal control, closed-loop ecology, or remote-sensing imaging) but also reveals its evolution and design implications in terrestrial physical applications, highlighting how "space logic" is translated into the product thinking of "earthly everyday life." Filtered through the two dimensions of "creative divergence" and "future adaptability," the corpus emphasizes identifying the "space genes" in civilian products and their potential for redesign. This pack suits "reverse innovation" modules in innovation-design education, product-redesign training, and cross-scenario speculative practice from a culture-technology fusion perspective.

**HT-01｜Memory Foam**

Description: This foam was originally developed by NASA for shuttle seat cushioning, to reduce the impact of high-speed acceleration on the human body. Its high energy absorption and slow rebound later led to commercialization in mattresses, pillows, insoles, and sports protective gear. Memory foam embodies the "space safety technology → civilian comfort product" translation path and is a classic case of technology feeding back into life on Earth.

Source: Wikipedia contributors. (2024). NASA spin-off technologies. Wikipedia. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA_spin-off_technologies

Category: Materials Technology; Keywords: shock absorption & comfort, space translation, mattress innovation

**HT-02｜Dry Shampoo Technology**

Description: To address washing and hygiene challenges in spaceflight, zero-gravity, or severely water-limited environments (such as the International Space Station), the Japanese company Kao Corporation — building on a topic submitted to the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) — developed the water-free "3D Space Shampoo Sheet." The product has entered ISS use and has been brought to Earth-side travel, outdoor, and emergency scenarios. The technology embodies a dual path of "resource-saving design → portable civilian hygiene product" across both extreme environments and daily life.

Source: Kao Corporation. (2023). Launch of Space Shampoo Sheet developed for astronauts on ISS. https://www.kao.com/global/en/newsroom/news/release/2023/20230328-001/

Category: Daily-Life Products; Keywords: dry shampoo, resource saving, portable hair care

**HT-03｜Scratch-Resistant Lens Coating**

Description: In space, a spacecraft must withstand high-speed micrometeoroid impacts, extreme temperature swings, and ultraviolet radiation; to enhance lens durability, NASA developed a high-hardness scratch-resistant coating technology. It was later translated to civilian eyeglasses, phone screens, and camera lenses, making them more wear-resistant and durable. This case demonstrates a technology-migration path between "aerospace materials" and "consumer display components."

Source: Interesting Engineering. (2023). 23 Great NASA Spin-off Technologies. Interesting Engineering. https://interestingengineering.com/science/23-great-nasa-spin-off-technologies

Category: Vision Aids; Keywords: scratch-resistant coating, lens upgrade, wear-resistant materials

**HT-04｜Cordless Vacuum "DustBuster"**

Description: During the Apollo lunar missions, to meet astronauts' need for portable, cordless, battery-powered tools during surface and extravehicular work, the power-tool company Black & Decker drew on its research into lunar drilling and cordless motors to develop portable battery-driven tools. That experience was later translated into the household cordless vacuum DustBuster, illustrating the path of "space tool design → portable home-cleaning equipment" in civilian life.

Source: NASA. (2003). Spinoff from a Moon Tool. NASA Technical Reports Server. https://ntrs.nasa.gov/api/citations/20030005114/downloads/20030005114.pdf

Category: Home Appliances; Keywords: cordless vacuuming, space tools, home-cleaning innovation

**HT-05｜High-Efficiency Solar Panels**

Description: To meet space probes' need for lightweight, high-efficiency solar modules, NASA vigorously advanced solar-cell technology, including multi-junction III-V cells, flexible thin-film cells, and lightweight stacked/foldable modules. These key technologies were subsequently translated and partly applied to terrestrial photovoltaic systems, laying a foundation for higher conversion efficiency and lower-cost pathways. The case embodies the "aerospace energy technology → terrestrial renewable energy" translation chain.

Source: NASA. (n.d.). Solar Cells. NASA Spinoff. https://spinoff.nasa.gov/node/9493

Category: Energy Systems; Keywords: photovoltaic conversion, high-efficiency solar, renewable energy

**HT-06｜CMOS Image Sensor**

Description: To meet spacecraft and probe imaging systems' need for miniaturization, low power, and high integration, JPL scientist Eric R. Fossum and colleagues developed Active-Pixel Sensor (APS) CMOS image-sensor technology in the early 1990s, giving sensor chips smaller size, lower power, and higher integration. The technology was then widely adopted in smartphone cameras, digital cameras, and surveillance/security systems, representing a model translation from "space-exploration intelligent imaging" to "consumer-electronics imaging."

Source: NASA. (2017). CMOS Sensors Enable Phone Cameras, HD Video. NASA Spinoff. https://spinoff.nasa.gov/Spinoff2017/cg_1.html

Category: Visual Imaging; Keywords: camera chip, smartphone imaging, image sensor

**HT-07｜Precision GPS Software**

Description: In the development of global positioning and navigation (GPS), NASA (via JPL) developed GIPSY-OASIS and its real-time version, Real-Time GIPSY, to model and correct satellite clock drift, atmospheric delay, and orbital error in real time, achieving centimeter-level accuracy. The software was later licensed for agricultural automation, terrestrial precision surveying, and emergency-response positioning, with potential to extend into autonomous-driving systems. It embodies the "aerospace navigation algorithm → terrestrial precision positioning service" translation path.

Source: NASA. (2019). Highly Accurate GPS Is Possible Thanks to NASA. https://www.nasa.gov/technology/tech-transfer-spinoffs/highly-accurate-gps-is-possible-thanks-nasa/

Category: Positioning Systems; Keywords: precision navigation, precise GPS, autonomous-driving assistance

**HT-08｜Aerogel Insulation**

Description: To insulate and reduce structural mass, NASA developed aerogel, an ultra-light insulating material with extremely low thermal conductivity and high temperature tolerance. It has been translated into building insulation, cold-chain transport, and outdoor gear, greatly improving energy efficiency and thermal management. It is a classic case of "aerospace insulation material → terrestrial energy-saving insulation."

Source: NASA. (2015). NASA Spinoffs: Improving Life on Earth. NASA. https://www.nasa.gov/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/167752main_fs_spinoffs508c.pdf

Category: Building / Insulation Materials; Keywords: aerogel, energy-saving insulation, lightweight materials

**HT-09｜Robo-Glove**

Description: Developed by NASA in collaboration with General Motors for ISS missions, this robotic assistive glove reduces wrist strain and enhances fine motor control. The technology was later turned by Bioservo Technologies into the IronHand industrial assistive glove, used for tasks such as bolt-tightening and pressure-relief operations in factories. It embodies the "space human-robot collaboration technology → industrial assistive equipment" translation path.

Source: Wired. (2020). NASA's Newest Spinoff Tech Comes Back to Earth. Wired. https://www.wired.com/story/nasas-newest-spinoff-tech-comes-back-to-earth/

Category: Assistive Equipment; Keywords: robotic glove, industrial collaboration, human-machine co-creation

The technology was later applied to home water purifiers, portable purification devices, and disaster-relief water treatment. It embodies the "aerospace water-recycling system → home water-purification innovation" path.

**HT-11｜Space-Grade Fire-Resistant Fabric**

Description: To safeguard spacecraft, spacesuits, and crewed-mission equipment under extreme conditions (high heat, oxidizing atmospheres, high radiation, intense flame exposure), NASA worked with Celanese Corporation to develop polybenzimidazole (PBI) fiber — a material with extremely high heat resistance, flame retardance, chemical resistance, and abrasion resistance. It was later translated and widely used in firefighting suits, industrial protective clothing, racing suits, and other high-risk workwear, embodying the "extreme-environment safety technology → terrestrial high-hazard protection" path while improving material performance and providing new safeguards for civilian safety.

Source: NASA. (2015). NASA Spinoffs: Improving Life on Earth. NASA. https://www.nasa.gov/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/167752main_fs_spinoffs508c.pdf

Category: Functional Apparel; Keywords: fire-resistant fiber, extreme-environment clothing, civilian safety

**HT-12｜Portable Solar Charging Backpack**

Description: To meet deep-space exploration's need for lightweight, high-efficiency solar panels, researchers developed efficient foldable solar curtains and lightweight energy-storage modules, later translated into travel/outdoor backpacks that can charge phones and laptops where no power source is available. **HT-13｜Smart Breathing-Sensor Mattress**

Description: For astronaut health monitoring in microgravity and closed environments, NASA developed and studied flexible thin-film sensor platforms that monitor respiration rate, heart rate, and other physiological metrics in real time. Smart-bedding makers later embedded such sensing into mattresses, offering sleep-quality analysis, breathing-anomaly alerts, and unobtrusive monitoring. The case embodies the "space vital-sign monitoring → home smart-bedding for health" path.

Such technology offered design inspiration for civilian education-robot makers, including companion/learning robots with mobility, grasping, and interactive voice for children's educational play, emphasizing collaboration, exploration, and interaction. The case shows the "space assistive-robot technology → educational companion robot" route.

**HT-15｜Home Workout System (from Compact Space-Gym Module)**

Description: To counter muscle/bone loss aboard spacecraft, NASA developed resistance-training systems (such as iRED and its elastic-disc technology) for efficient strength training in microgravity. These technologies were later licensed for terrestrial home fitness equipment (such as the Bowflex Revolution series) and, combined with foldable/storable structures, pushed home gear toward "efficient / space-saving" designs. The case embodies the "space extreme-training equipment → efficient home fitness product" trend.

Source: NASA. (2019). Bowflex System Spurs Revolution in Home Fitness. NASA Spinoff. https://spinoff.nasa.gov/Spinoff2019/cg_1.html

**HT-16｜Civil Emergency Pack (from Space-Medicine Emergency Kit)**

Description: Medical kits for space missions must be lightweight, versatile, capable of remote diagnosis, and able to automatically resupply medication. Such extreme low-temperature cold-storage management is now extending to terrestrial portable preservation / pharmaceutical cold-chain systems, suited to outdoor food storage, field research, and medicine transport in remote areas. The case embodies the "space cold-storage management → terrestrial portable preservation equipment" path.

**HT-20｜Emergency Shelter (Space Habitat Inflate-Tech → Emergency Shelter)**

Description: To improve the rapid-deployment capability of spacecraft or planetary-surface habitats, inflatable/deployable structural technologies (such as multilayer composite membranes and lightweight foldable shells) were developed for space; these designs are being adapted for terrestrial disaster-relief tents, field inflatable shelters, and flexible-structure carports, embodying the "space deployable structure → terrestrial emergency architecture" path.

**HT-21｜Capillary-Driven Heat Pipes**

Description: In microgravity, conventional heat-exchange devices that rely on gravity or forced convection tend to fail, so NASA and others developed two-phase heat-transport systems based on capillary action (such as Capillary-Pumped Loops) for spacecraft cooling. The core idea transports heat through capillary structures in an evaporation-condensation cycle without gravity. Its principles (lightweight, passive cooling, high efficiency) have been introduced into terrestrial electronics cooling (high-performance laptops, power-dense lighting, medical heat-dissipation modules), offering ideas for thermal-management challenges.

Source: Lekan, J. F., & Allen, J. S. (1998). Capillary-Driven Heat Transfer Experiment: Keeping It Cool in Space. NASA Technical Reports Server. https://ntrs.nasa.gov/api/citations/20050177238/downloads/20050177238.pdf

Category: Cooling Components; Keywords: gravity-free heat exchange, laptop cooling, energy-saving technology, space thermal control

**HT-22｜Space-Formulated Hydrogel Dressing**

Description: To address slow wound healing and reduced immune function in space, NASA and private partners studied a hydrogel dressing that maintains a moist environment and provides a tissue-regeneration scaffold and antimicrobial drug delivery. The technology has begun to be promoted for terrestrial medical wound dressings, used in surgical care, burn treatment, and everyday wound management.

Source: NASA Spinoff. (2019). Hydrogel wound dressing goes from space station to home. https://spinoff.nasa.gov/Spinoff2019/hm_2.html

Category: Medical Dressings; Keywords: wound healing, hydrogel, antimicrobial care, aerospace-translated medicine

Description: Derived from satellite Earth-observation technology (such as multispectral imaging from the Sentinel series under Europe's Copernicus programme), these "space-grade" imaging capabilities, combined with ground-based farmland monitoring, are used in precision agriculture: crop-growth monitoring, irrigation control, moisture/chlorophyll-index analysis, and early identification of pests or disease, greatly improving efficiency and reducing resource waste.

Source: European Space Agency / Copernicus Programme — Land services (Agriculture). https://www.esa.int/Applications/Observing_the_Earth/Copernicus/Land_services

Category: Agricultural Remote Sensing; Keywords: precision agriculture, satellite monitoring, crop management, aerospace-image translation

Such technology was later introduced into high-intensity sports and training gear — athlete cooling vests, post-game recovery suits — to delay heat stress and heatstroke risk and improve overall heat-load tolerance.

The concept is drawing interest in terrestrial footwear design to improve walking stability and reduce injury risk in hiking and outdoor shoes.
