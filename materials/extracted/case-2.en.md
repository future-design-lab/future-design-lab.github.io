Case Library 2: Cultural Structure & Ritual Evolution

This case library focuses on the theme of "cultural structure and ritual evolution": how can the ritual structures and emotional codes of Earth's civilization be extended into a spacefaring civilization? It collects cases concerned with directions such as "ritualized design," "cultural structure," "digital faith," and "collective emotion," providing references for the design of ritual and cultural systems in space civilization.

**CR-01｜Moon Temple ／ Peak of Eternal Light**

Description: This project was proposed by artist Jorge Mañes Rubio during his tenure as artist-in-residence at the European Space Agency (ESA). The vision is to build a "Moon Temple" at the lunar south pole (near the edge of Shackleton Crater) using 3D-printed lunar regolith, including a temple structure, a ritual space, and an astronomical telescope installation.

Insight: This design extracts the "religious/ritual site" from Earth's culture and places it in an environment such as the Moon that has "no land" and "no ancestors," rethinking how ritual is constructed. It extracts the cultural genes of "temple," "assembly," "astronomical observation," and "commemoration," and re-encodes them in an extreme environment. In the absence of traditional land and ancestral anchors, a "ritual site" can be re-established through space, form, and symbolism, thereby extending cultural genes.

Technology: • Lighting Control: through architectural openings and structural layout, natural sunlight becomes part of the design. ; • Sensing Technology: at the conceptual level, environmental conditions, temperature differences, and geological feedback are considered to guide the spatial construction. ; • IoT / Smart Environment: potentially extended into an interactive architectural prototype for future lunar habitation systems that responds to the environment and human activity.

Design Object: • Architecture: using the "temple" as a spatial carrier to redefine what "building" and "shelter" mean on the Moon. ; • Installation: a series of sculptures and "ritual objects" made from lunar soil simulant, forming tangible spiritual symbols. ; • Lighting / Lightscape: sited at the "Peak of Eternal Light," using natural light and shadow to compose a ritualistic spatial experience.

Design Method: • Transference: transferring the concept of the "temple," which carries spiritual and ritual meaning on Earth, to a lunar context, exploring the regeneration of culture and faith in a completely unfamiliar environment. ; • Narrativization: using the "Moon Temple" story as a framework to construct a narrative of future humans establishing a spiritual home on the Moon. ; • Ritualization: through the symbolic expression of architectural form and material, it simulates the ritual experience of Earth's civilization, evoking contemplation and shared emotion. ; • Contrast: using the stark contrasts between Earth and Moon, technological rationality and humanistic spirit, light and darkness, to create tension and mystery.

Future Impact: provides a visualizable, discussable paradigm for "ritual" and "cultural sites" in a space civilization; it suggests that even in a new environment with no ancestors and no traditional land, humans will still seek gathering, symbols, and spatial ritual.

Principle: reconstruction of ritual sites / abstraction + re-encoding of cultural genes / shared space and gathering meaning even without a geographic ancestral anchor

**CR-02｜Machines as Collaborators for Art and Rituals**

Description: This is a research project published by CM Fang et al. in 2025, exploring the possibility of "machines as ritual collaborators." The research designed collaborative AI systems, sensors, and VR environments to "co-construct" ritual behaviors.

Insight: It shows that ritual need not be a fixed tradition but can interact with technology and become part of the design process. For the space environment, a "ritual generator" can be regarded as a technological + cultural tool, where AI and sensors can assist in generating and evolving rituals.

Technology: • Biofeedback: real-time capture of EEG brainwaves, motion signals, and breathing rhythms, fed back into visual and audio output. ; • AI Generation: using an RNN to learn and generate brushstrokes and visual styles from datasets of the artist's past works. ; • Robotic Interaction: a multi-robotic-arm system participates in painting, responding to human movement and algorithmic instructions. ; • Audio System: sound is part of the ritual, changing in sync with movement and meditative states. ; • Sensing Technology: cameras, position tracking, EEG sensors, etc., used to record bodily and environmental feedback in real time. ; • Immersive Technology: a meditation-like immersive experience is created through spatial light and shadow and a multisensory layout.

Design Object: • Interactive System: an installation in which humans and a multi-robotic-arm system draw together in real time, such as Drawing Operations Unit Gen. 1–3. ; • Installation: Assembly Lines is an immersive performance installation combining motion capture, sound, painting, and meditation. ; • Soundscape: using EEG feedback and motion signals to drive a live audio environment, forming a synchronized auditory layer. ; • AI / Generative System: a neural network learns the artist's brushstroke patterns and generates painting output with its own style. ; • Wearable / Bio-design: using EEG sensors to record brainwaves during meditation, reverse-driving visual and audio feedback.

Design Method: • Embodiment: Chung produces real-time resonance with the machine system through physiological signals such as body movement, brainwaves, and meditative states, making creation a joint act of body–algorithm–environment. ; • Ritualization: works such as Assembly Lines open with meditation, fusing performance, painting, and sound into a shared ritual experience, exploring how to rebuild "community" moments after technology interrupts ritual. ; • Generative: by training a recurrent neural network (RNN) model, the AI generates new brushstrokes and rhythms based on the artist's years of hand-drawn traces, forming a self-evolving painting style. ; • Synesthesia: the work combines sound, vision, and physiological data to create a cross-sensory hybrid experience, making meditation and painting mutually map onto each other visually and aurally. ; • Intervention: the artist transforms the machine from a "tool" into a "co-creator," using behavioral empathy and system errors as opportunities to intervene in the audience's perception of the "human–machine boundary."

Future Impact: this research implies that future rituals will be more "collaborative" and "machine-participatory" in character, which is especially meaningful in environments with no geographic anchor and no common language (such as a space capsule).

Principle: Cultural Techniques — emphasizing the operationalized mechanisms of how culture functions.

Source: Academic paper "Machines as Collaborators for Art and Rituals"; https://doi.org/10.1145/3596928

**CR-03｜New Rituals**

Description: A design project published on the Future Architecture Platform, in which the author, during the COVID-19 lockdown, created 7 objects from discarded materials at home (cardboard, rope, etc.), each representing a "ritual"—related to fire, water, and reflection. The author emphasized that "we focus on everyday rituals rather than creating a new religion."

Insight: It emphasizes that ritual can be rooted in "routine / the rhythm of life" rather than necessarily relying on traditional religion or geographic-cultural background. In the space environment, there is no land or ancestors to rely on, but "everyday rituals" + "bodily behavior" + "symbolic transformation" can still establish ritual. For example, replacing "lighting a candle" with "activating a light source," or replacing "burial" with "spatial commemoration"—this case is a small-scale transformation model.

Technology: • Sensing Technology: relying on materials (the reflectivity of an emergency blanket, the changing light and shadow of a candle) to sense spatial changes and bodily states. ; • Lighting Control: using candlelight, silver reflective material, and discarded objects to produce dynamic light-and-shadow effects, reinforcing the ritual atmosphere. ; • Data Interface / IoT (extensibility): although no digital system is directly used, the logic of "turning household materials into a ritual site" has the potential to be upgraded into a smart space.

Design Object: • Installation: seven ritual objects the author made at home from discarded items, each corresponding to a material (such as a wooden branch, a sponge, a brick) and a form of ritual (fire, water, reflection). ; • Architecture: transforming the apartment into a temporary contemplative space, enveloping the whole space with a silver emergency blanket, redefining the home environment as a ritual site.

Design Method: • Ritualization: transforming repetitive behaviors in everyday life (such as fire, water, reflection) into ritual objects of a contemplative nature. ; • Contrast: using household materials (cardboard, tape, brick, emergency blanket) to create visual and material conflict with the symbols of traditional ritual space (sacred / silent), reinforcing the experiential tension of a crisis state. ; • Synesthesia: the space wrapped in a silver emergency blanket reflects light and candlelight, producing a cross-sensory experience in the relationship between light, object, and shadow.

Future Impact: it provides a "low-threshold ritual generation" path: even after losing cultural roots and having space reconstructed, humans can still create shared rituals through simple objects + behaviors.

Principle: Ritual Design—with symbols, behavior, and rhythm at its core.

**CR-05｜MoonArk (a lunar "micro-museum" payload)**

MoonArk launched together with CMU's small lunar rover IRIS aboard the Astrobotic Peregrine lander on January 8, 2024; although the mission's landing subsequently failed, MoonArk's structure, contents, vehicle integration, and "lunar museum" methodology are all fully publicly documented.

Design Object: • Interactive System: the Iris lunar rover is a controllable robotic system designed by a faculty-and-student team, intended to perform operational tasks on the lunar surface. ; • Installation: MoonArk is an art / cultural installation module composed of titanium, platinum, sapphire, etc., containing images, poems, music, nano-objects, and more, carrying the cultural memory of Earth. ; • Architecture: in the lunar-surface field formed by the lander + payload, Iris and MoonArk together form a composite "cultural + technological" space.
