Case Library 3: Time Reconstruction & Emotional Connection

This case library focuses on the theme of "Time Reconstruction and Emotional Connection": when circadian rhythms and seasonal changes break down, how can design rebuild emotion, memory, and ritual? It collects cases concerned with "time perception," "emotional interaction," "digital memorialization," and related directions, offering references for the design of emotion and memory after the loss of Earth's temporal anchors.

**TE-01｜Shorter than the Day（Sarah Sze，2020）**

Description: The work was created by artist Sarah Sze and installed suspended in the baggage claim area of LaGuardia Airport Terminal B in New York. Using approximately 900 photographs of the New York sky from dawn to dusk as material, the piece is composed of a spherical structure and suspension rods. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shorter_than_the_Day?utm_source=chatgpt.com The artist describes it as "the sensation of shifting time and place"—as viewers move around the work, they see images of the sky transitioning from day to night, capturing the passage of time.

Insight: When normal references such as "day and night" and "the seasons" (the sky, sunlight) become ineffective or blurred in certain environments (such as space, polar regions, or artificial settings), design uses photography and structure to freeze and replay "the passage of time." The familiarity of perception: people are accustomed to perceiving time through "changes in the sky" and "sunrise and sunset," but when these references are absent, the work builds an alternative mechanism using "a sequence of photographs plus a spatial structure," allowing viewers to "experience" time rather than merely "observe" it. The work can function as a kind of "ritual": people circle, observe, and reflect on time and the moment, on memory and disappearance.

Technology: Structural and suspension systems; photographic and image composition; lighting and spatial control technologies

Design Object: Installation: a suspended spherical structure plus a photographic system.; Spatial Experience: the viewer's circulation path as a temporal narrative path.

Design Method: Materialization: presenting "the material form of time" through photographs and structure.; Narrativization: telling the passage of time through changes in circulation and viewpoint.; Assemblage: combining 900 photographs into a continuous "sky-time."; Ritualization: the act of viewers circling and observing becomes "a ritual of experiencing time."

Future Impact: In environments without distinct day and night or with abnormal cycles (such as a lunar base), one might consider using a similar "temporal imagery plus spatial structure" to help people establish an emotional connection to "day/night" and "past/present." It reminds designers: when rhythmic references are broken, design can take on the task of re-creating "the experience of time," not merely compensating for function.

Principle: Time as structured visual material; space/photography/circulation jointly constructing a sense of time; mechanisms that substitute for natural rhythms

Source: Wikipedia entry; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shorter_than_the_Day

Tags: Present; P Individual Artist; Artistic; time perception, memory, ritual, day-night transition, structured temporal experience

**TE-02｜Atemporal Memory（Interactive Architecture Lab）**

Description: This is a work built on VR / immersive environments. The project uses wireless headsets and multiple depth cameras, enabling users to interact with their own "virtual past selves," fast-forward / rewind, and experience past and present simultaneously within the same space. https://www.interactivearchitecture.org/atemporal-memory-time-perception-and-media-temporality.html?utm_source=chatgpt.com The project sets up multiple mirror walls, one of which provides real-time feedback while the others provide delayed feedback, so that participants experience a dislocation "between body, action, and time" within the space.

Insight: In environments where temporal rhythms (day/night, seasons) break down or are hard to distinguish (for example, polar regions, enclosed environments, space capsules), the body may lose its "sunrise–sunset" reference. This project offers an experience of "time as an operable / perceivable material." Through interaction design, users not only observe the passage of time but "manipulate time," coexisting with their past selves, thereby rebuilding the connection of "memory—present—body." It can be read as a kind of "ritual": traversing, rewinding, and conversing with oneself to rebuild a sense of time and of the body.

Technology: VR and depth cameras; real-time delayed imaging system; perceptual tracking and feedback algorithms

Design Object: Immersive VR Installation; Interactive Mirror System

Design Method: Simulation: reproducing "temporal dislocation" through delayed imagery.; Embodiment: the body coexisting with its past self in the same space.; Interactive Narrative: letting users manipulate time.; Memory Reenactment: experiencing the cycle of one's own time within the space.

Future Impact: In space or extreme environments, similar VR / augmented reality methods could help people maintain emotion, memory, and time perception, mitigating the effects of failed day-night cycles. Designers might consider "manipulable time devices" as tools for emotional / memory support.

Principle: The passage of time structured into interactive elements; the user becoming the operator of time; a triple reconstruction of body–time–space perception

Source: Interactive Architecture Lab project page; https://www.interactivearchitecture.org/atemporal-memory-time-perception-and-media-temporality.html

**TE-17｜Moon Ensō (Engessō) by Miya Ando（新加坡/国际，2025）**

Description: This is an installation artwork created by Miya Ando, titled Moon Ensō (Engessō), exhibited at ART SG in Singapore in 2025. For theme #3: when the traditional day-night, solar illumination, and geographic time are weakened (or converted into lunar-phase time), design can use this "cyclical / suspension" and "walking / viewing" mechanism to establish memory nodes and emotional connection.

Future Impact: It provides inspiration for temporal structures that substitute for the "Earth-day rhythm" in space or no-day-night environments; it suggests "using natural cycles to replace everyday time" to establish a sense of ritual.
